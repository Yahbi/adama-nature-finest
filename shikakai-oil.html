Amla, shikakai and bhringraj, warmed and worked in; the old pre-wash rite.

  • Amla fruit is recognised for a constituent profile of hydrolysable tannins, flavonoids, saponins, terpenoids, alkaloids and ascorbic acid, alongside minerals and amino acids. The review that collects this record notes that much of the pharmacological work on the fruit lacks dose standards, controls and stated mechanism.1
  • More than one hundred and eighty compounds have been isolated and identified from the fruit, chiefly tannins, phenolic acids, flavonoids, terpenoids, polysaccharides, fatty acids and amino acids.2
  • Polyphenols and vitamin C are the constituents at the centre of the fruit, and the ones its measured antioxidant activity is attributed to. That activity is measured in assay, not in skin.3
  • Shikakai pod is recognised for triterpenoid saponins — plant surfactants — built on the acacic acid backbone. Chromatographic separation and LC-MS place the pod's active fraction as a mixture of such saponins, carrying a monoterpene unit and five sugars: glucose, xylose, rhamnose, quinovose and arabinose.4
  • The saponin fraction of the pod has been resolved structurally: five prosapogenols, the concinnosides, were isolated from its alkaline hydrolysate, alongside the known glycosides acaciaside, julibroside A1, julibroside A3 and albiziasaponin C, and the aglycone acacic acid lactone.5
  • Three further acacic-acid saponins, the kinmoonosides, have been isolated and elucidated from the fruit. The record here is structural chemistry; it establishes what the pod is made of and nothing beyond that.6

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Hydrolysable tannins (Phyllanthus emblica, fruit)
The characteristic constituent class of amla. Gallic and ellagic acid sit in the same phenolic fraction.
Polyphenols and ascorbic acid (Phyllanthus emblica, fruit)
The constituents the fruit's assay-measured antioxidant activity is attributed to.
Triterpenoid saponins (Acacia concinna, fruit)
Acacic-acid saponins — plant surfactants. This is the molecule class shikakai is known by. The INCI designation is Acacia Concinna Fruit Extract; the accepted botanical name in current taxonomy is Senegalia rugata (Lam.) Britton & Rose.
Cocos Nucifera Oil
Coconut oil. With sesame it forms the oil phase the extracts are carried in. No verified literature is held for this material; it therefore carries no property statement above.
Sesamum Indicum Seed Oil
Sesame oil. The second component of the oil phase. No verified literature is held for this material.
Eclipta Prostrata Extract
Bhringraj. Named on the deck and in the traditional formula. No verified literature is held for this material; it therefore carries no property statement above.
Tocopherol
Carried in the oil phase, against oxidation.

Full ingredients — INCI

Vessel
Cylindrical dropper bottle, 100 mL, with collar and bulb.
Material
Stoneware body; the dropper assembly is a separate part.
Recyclability
The stoneware is fired clay — kept and refilled rather than recycled. Separate the dropper from the bottle before disposal — the assembly is a mixed part.

Storage. Keep closed, upright, away from light and heat. Plant oils oxidise; the opaque stoneware slows it, the cap does the rest.

Precautions. For external use. Avoid the eye area. Shampoo out; this is a pre-wash oil and is not left in. This oil is fragranced. Patch test before first use and discontinue if irritation occurs. Two things about Acacia concinna belong here rather than in the properties above. The Cosmetic Ingredient Review expert panel assessed Acacia Concinna Fruit Extract and concluded the available data were insufficient to support its safety in cosmetics, naming skin sensitisation and contact urticaria among the gaps (PMID 16422266). Separately, a case of occupational IgE-mediated respiratory allergy to shikakai powder has been published (PMID 41386511) — a single case, by the powder-handling route rather than the cutaneous one. Neither finding is a claim against the material; both are the honest state of the record, and we would rather print them than not.
