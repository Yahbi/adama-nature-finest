Shikakai Cleansing Powder

Dosage
Two tablespoons per wash, mixed fresh.
  • The shikakai pod is recognised for a highly polar saponin fraction. Alkaline hydrolysis of it yielded five triterpenoid prosapogenols — concinnosides A to E — alongside the known glycosides acaciaside, julibroside A1, julibroside A3 and albiziasaponin C, all built on the same triterpene aglycone, acacic acid.1
  • Later chromatographic separation and LC-MS confirmed the pod's active fraction as a mixture of saponins on that acacic acid backbone, each carrying a monoterpene acyl moiety and sugar units drawn from glucose, xylose, rhamnose, quinovose and arabinose. Saponins are glycosides with a lipophilic core and a sugar tail — the structure that lets a plant foam in water.3
  • Three further acacic-acid saponins, kinmoonosides A to C, were isolated and structurally elucidated from the fruit, together with a new monoterpenoid.2
  • Amla fruit is recognised for an unusually crowded phytochemistry: more than 180 compounds have been isolated from it, principally tannins, phenolic acids, flavonoids, terpenoids, polysaccharides, fatty acids and amino acids.6
  • Polyphenols and ascorbic acid are the constituents at the centre of the amla fruit, and the measured antioxidant activity of the fruit is attributed to that pairing.7
  • Reetha is recognised for triterpenoidal saponins of the oleanane, dammarane and tirucallane types as its principal identified constituent class — the molecules the fruit shell has been washed with for as long as it has been picked.8

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Triterpenoid saponins
The dominant constituent class of the shikakai pod, and the principal identified class of the reetha fruit. Glycosides with a lipophilic core and a sugar tail.
Acacic acid
The triterpene aglycone — the sapogenin backbone the shikakai saponins are built on.
Concinnosides A–F
Prosapogenols isolated from the shikakai pod saponin fraction.
Kinmoonosides A–C
Acacic acid saponins isolated from the shikakai fruit.
Acaciaside, julibroside A1, julibroside A3, albiziasaponin C
Known glycosides identified in the shikakai pod.
Oleanane-, dammarane- and tirucallane-type saponins
The saponin types reported across Sapindus mukorossi.
Hydrolysable tannins
A principal constituent class of the amla fruit.
Gallic acid and ellagic acid
Phenolic acids of the amla fruit.
Ascorbic acid
Present in the amla fruit; with the polyphenols, the pairing the fruit's measured antioxidant activity is attributed to.
Flavonoids
Quercetin and kaempferol glycosides, reported in the amla fruit.
Alkaloids and malic acid
Reported alongside saponins as general constituents of Acacia concinna fruit extract.

Full ingredients — INCI

Acacia Concinna Fruit Powder, Emblica Officinalis Fruit Powder, Sapindus Mukorossi Fruit Powder, Eclipta Prostrata Leaf Powder.

Vessel
Stoneware jar, 100 g
Material
Stoneware jar with a cork lid.
Recyclability
Stoneware jar with a cork lid — kept and refilled rather than recycled. The powder carries no pump or dropper to separate.

Storage. Keep the jar closed, dry, away from direct sunlight and heat. Water is what starts this product; keep it out of the jar and mix only what is to be used.

Precautions. For external use. Mix into water rather than working the powder dry, and do not raise dust: a case of occupational IgE-mediated respiratory allergy to shikakai powder has been published, arising from powder handling by inhalation rather than from washing. The Cosmetic Ingredient Review expert panel assessed Acacia Concinna Fruit Extract for cosmetic use and concluded the available data were insufficient to support its safety, naming gaps that include chemical characterisation, skin sensitisation dose-response and contact urticaria. That is an open question in the formal literature, not a finding against the material — but it is the honest state of it. Patch test before first use. Avoid contact with the eyes; rinse with water if it occurs. Do not use on broken or inflamed scalp. Discontinue if irritation occurs. Keep out of reach of children.
