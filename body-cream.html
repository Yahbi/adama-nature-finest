How to use
Massage into clean, dry skin morning and night, focusing on rough or dry areas.
  • Shea butter is recognised for a lipid fraction built principally of triacylglycerols — chiefly SOS, the 1,3-distearoyl-2-oleoyl form — with stearic and oleic acid the two dominant fatty acids. Which of the two dominates is not fixed: across 150 butters surveyed from four African countries, stearic acid led in West African material and oleic acid led in Ugandan.1
  • The same survey reports the butter's distinctive unsaponifiable constituents — acetyl and cinnamyl polycyclic triterpene esters — at country means of roughly 3.7 to 12.6 per cent of the fat. It is an unusually large minor fraction for a plant butter, and it is what the chemistry of shea is known by.1
  • Crude shea butter is recognised for carrying tocopherols, phytosterols, phenolics and flavonoids within its unsaponifiable matter, with stearic and oleic acid confirmed as the two principal fatty acids. Refining lowers those minor components.2
  • The kernel and the butter are not the same material. Eight catechins, gallic acid, quercetin and trans-cinnamic acid have been identified in shea kernels, with catechins at a mean of some 4000 ppm of kernel — but only about 97 ppm of total polyphenols carry through into the butter itself. The figure that belongs on a butter is the second one.3
  • Shea butter is among the vegetable butters that have been examined in clinical studies of dermal use — on unaffected skin, in xerosis and in atopic dermatitis. The review that collects them draws its conclusions at the level of fatty-acid class rather than of shea specifically.4

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Triacylglycerols, principally SOS
The bulk of the butter: 1,3-distearoyl-2-oleoyl-glycerol.
Stearic acid and oleic acid
The two dominant fatty acids of shea butter. Their ratio varies with provenance.
Acetyl and cinnamyl polycyclic triterpene esters
The butter's characteristic unsaponifiable constituents; country means of roughly 3.7–12.6 per cent of the fat.
Tocopherols and phytosterols
Minor components of the unsaponifiable matter of crude butter.
Urea
A small hydrophilic molecule; here at humectant use.
Glycerin
Humectant.
Panthenol
Provitamin B5; the alcohol analogue of pantothenic acid.
Cetearyl Alcohol and Glyceryl Stearate
Fatty alcohol and emulsifier; they hold the emulsion together.
Xanthan Gum
Polysaccharide thickener.

Full ingredients — INCI

Aqua, Glycerin, Urea, Butyrospermum Parkii Butter, Cetearyl Alcohol, Glyceryl Stearate, Panthenol, Xanthan Gum, Benzyl Alcohol, Dehydroacetic Acid, Parfum.

Declarable fragrance allergens

Benzyl Alcohol — declared on the deck and among the 26 substances listed in Annex III of EC 1223/2009. The Parfum declares no further allergens of that list.

Vessel
200 mL tube
Recyclability
Tubes are emptied but rarely rinsed clean, and the cap is usually a different polymer from the sleeve. Squeeze the tube out, replace the cap, and check that your local stream accepts tubes before adding it.

Storage. Keep the tube closed. Store away from direct sunlight and heat.

Precautions. For external use. Avoid the eye area. Shea is the nut of a Sapotaceae tree; those who avoid tree nuts may wish to note it. Urea can sting on broken or fissured skin. This cream is fragranced. Discontinue if irritation occurs.
