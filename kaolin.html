White Kaolin

$16 200 g

The gentlest of the clays; a weekly reset for skin that dislikes drama.

The softest cosmetic clay there is — fine, white, barely drawing. The weekly mask for skin that finds green clay loud.

Key ingredients
  • White Kaolin
How to use
One spoon with water or hydrosol into a cream; five minutes, then rinse.
Botanical name
Kaolinite — Al2Si2O5(OH)4
Function
Absorbent; bulking agent (cosmetic functions as recorded by the Cosmetic Ingredient Review). Also described as an adsorbent, opacifier and viscosity-increasing agent in clay-silicate formulation.
Also known as
White clay, Kaolinite, China clay, Argile blanche, Hydrated aluminium silicate, CI 77004
How to use
A weekly mask. One spoonful into a small bowl, water or a hydrosol added a little at a time until it turns to a smooth cream. Spread thinly over clean skin, leave five minutes, and rinse before it dries and tightens. Not intended for the eye area.
Dosage
One spoonful of clay to roughly one spoonful of liquid, adjusted until the paste holds. Approximately 5 g per application; a Stoneware jar, 200 g is around forty masks.
In practice
Mix in glass, ceramic or plastic, with a wooden or plastic spoon; metal is conventionally avoided when working clays. Prepare only what is needed — the paste is intended to be made fresh and used at once, not stored wet. Rinse away with tepid water rather than allowing it to set hard on the skin. As a dry powder it can be dispersed into a cleansing paste, or blended with other clays where a gentler proportion is wanted.
  • Kaolin is a hydrous aluminium silicate, Al2Si2O5(OH)4 — a 1:1 phyllosilicate in which a single tetrahedral silica sheet is bound to a single octahedral alumina sheet, forming pseudohexagonal platelets typically under two micrometres across. Each platelet carries two faces of opposite charge: a negatively charged siloxane face and a positively charged aluminol face.1
  • The Cosmetic Ingredient Review Expert Panel records the function of naturally-sourced clays, kaolin among them, as absorbents and bulking agents, and concludes kaolin is safe in cosmetics at present practices of use and concentration.2
  • Clay silicates that contain structural water — kaolin included — are described in the cosmetic safety literature as functioning primarily as adsorbents, opacifiers and viscosity-increasing agents in formulation.3
  • The pharmaceutical literature records kaolinite's long-standing topical use as a dermatological protectant and in pelotherapy. This is a description of practice rather than a measured effect on skin.1
  • Commercial therapeutic clays whose principal minerals were kaolinite and illite have been measured as oil-absorptive and plastic enough to adhere to skin. The measurement belongs to the mineral class, not to this material.4

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Kaolinite
The mineral itself: a hydrous aluminium phyllosilicate, Al2Si2O5(OH)4.
1:1 dioctahedral layer structure
One tetrahedral silica sheet to one octahedral alumina sheet — the arrangement that distinguishes kaolinite from the swelling 2:1 clays.
Pseudohexagonal platelets
Typically under two micrometres in size.
Amphoteric surface
A negatively charged siloxane face and a positively charged aluminol face on the same platelet.
Low cation exchange capacity
Kaolinite exchanges few ions compared with the smectite clays; it neither swells nor takes water into an interlayer.

Full ingredients — INCI

Kaolin.

Vessel
Stoneware jar, 200 g

Storage. Kept dry, closed, and away from steam. Clay takes up moisture from the air; a jar left open in a bathroom will cake. Store the powder at room temperature and do not return mixed paste to it.

Precautions. For external use. Avoid the eye area, and do not allow the powder to become airborne — the documented pulmonary effects of kaolin arise from occupational inhalation of the dust, not from cosmetic use on skin, and cosmetic guidance is to formulate so that inhalation is minimised. No claim is made here that kaolin treats any condition of the skin.
