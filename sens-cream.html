How to use
Massage into dry skin as often as needed through the day.
  • Oat is recognised for a phytochemical set that a systematic review of 154 studies catalogued at 113 reported compounds. Two constituent classes are characteristic of the grain: beta-glucans — a soluble polysaccharide, reported significantly higher in oat than in buckwheat — and avenanthramides, the N-cinnamoylanthranilic acids that carry the plant's name. The review notes that extraction methods across the underlying studies were heterogeneous enough to preclude meta-analysis, so quantities belong to the literature as ranges, not as figures.1
  • The grain is also recognised for a steroidal saponin fraction. Avenacosides A and B were quantitated across fifteen commercial oat products at a combined 49.6 to 443.0 mg per kilogram — a wide spread, and a reminder that cultivar and milling decide it.2
  • Colloidal oatmeal — the whole kernel milled and held in suspension, which is the material declared on this deck as Avena Sativa Kernel Flour — is recognised in the dermatological literature for its use on dry skin. The study most often cited for it enrolled 50 subjects with bilateral dry skin on the lower legs and reported improvement in dryness, moisturisation and barrier measures. It was investigator-blinded but single-arm, with no vehicle control, and its authors were affiliated with a skincare manufacturer. It is a signal, not a proof, and none of it was measured on this formulation.3
  • A 1 per cent colloidal oatmeal cream has been examined in two further studies, in mild-to-moderate atopic dermatitis, over fourteen days. Both reported good tolerability alongside improvement in symptom and hydration measures. Both were small — 29 and 30 patients — single-arm, unblinded and industry-affiliated. What they establish most solidly is tolerability; the efficacy question they leave open.4
  • Shea butter is recognised for a lipid fraction built principally of triacylglycerols, with stearic and oleic acid the two dominant fatty acids. Which of the two leads is not fixed: across 150 butters surveyed from four African countries, stearic acid led in West African material and oleic acid led in Ugandan. The butter's distinctive unsaponifiables — acetyl and cinnamyl polycyclic triterpene esters — ran to country means of roughly 3.7 to 12.6 per cent of the fat.5

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Avena Sativa Kernel Flour (colloidal oatmeal)
The whole oat kernel milled and suspended, rather than an extract or an oil. This distinction decides which literature applies: the dermatological work on oat is almost entirely on colloidal oatmeal, and does not transfer to oat kernel oil.
Beta-glucans
The soluble polysaccharide fraction of the grain, reported significantly higher in oat than in buckwheat.
Avenanthramides
N-cinnamoylanthranilic acids characteristic of oat. The mechanistic work on these molecules is in vitro and in animals; nothing here is a statement about skin.
Avenacosides A and B
Steroidal furostanol saponins of the grain, reported at a combined 49.6–443.0 mg/kg across commercial oat products.
Butyrospermum Parkii Butter
Shea. Principally triacylglycerols, stearic and oleic acid dominant, carrying an unusually large unsaponifiable fraction of polycyclic triterpene esters. Vitellaria paradoxa is the accepted botanical name; Butyrospermum parkii is what INCI prints.
Glycerin
Humectant, and second on the deck.
Allantoin
A small heterocyclic molecule; here at skin-conditioning use.
Cetearyl Alcohol and Glyceryl Stearate
Fatty alcohol and emulsifier; they hold the emulsion together.
Xanthan Gum
Polysaccharide thickener.
Benzyl Alcohol and Dehydroacetic Acid
Preservative pair. Benzyl alcohol is carried here for preservation, not as a fragrance ingredient.

Full ingredients — INCI

Vessel
200 mL tube
Recyclability
Tubes are emptied but rarely rinsed clean, and the cap is usually a different polymer from the sleeve. Squeeze the tube out, replace the cap, and check that your local stream accepts tubes before adding it.

Storage. Keep the tube closed. Store away from direct sunlight and heat.

Precautions. For external use. Avoid the eye area. Contains oat, a cereal: contact sensitisation to colloidal oatmeal is documented, and anyone who avoids oats or gluten should note it before use. Contains shea, the nut of a Sapotaceae tree; those who avoid tree nuts may wish to note it. Patch test before first use. Discontinue if irritation occurs. Keep out of reach of children.
