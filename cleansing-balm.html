How to use
Massage onto dry skin. Add water to emulsify, then rinse or wipe away. For external use.
  • Sunflower seed oil is among the plant oils covered by the dermatological literature on topical lipids, where the essential fatty-acid ratio is described as a determinant of barrier behaviour: a higher linoleic-to-oleic ratio is associated with barrier-repair potential, and a higher oleic content is described as less favourable.1
  • In a small forearm-controlled study in nineteen adult volunteers, sunflower seed oil preserved stratum corneum integrity and did not induce erythema, where olive oil in the same study did not. Nineteen subjects is a mechanistic observation, not a proof, and it was not made on this product.2
  • Sunflower seed is reported to carry phenolic acids, flavonoids and tocopherols among its constituents.3
  • Jojoba is not an oil in the ordinary sense. Its seed reserve is liquid wax ester — long-chain fatty acids esterified to long-chain fatty alcohols — rather than the triacylglycerols of the sunflower oil and the shea butter beside it.4
  • The acid moieties of the jojoba esters are principally eicosenoic, erucic and oleic; tocopherols and phytosterols accompany them.5
  • Shea butter is reported as a triacylglycerol fat dominated by stearic and oleic acids, carrying acetyl and cinnamyl polycyclic triterpene esters at country means of roughly four to thirteen per cent. The balance of stearic to oleic acid varies with the region the kernels come from.6
  • The catechins of the shea kernel are reported at around 4000 ppm, but only about 97 ppm of total polyphenols are reported to carry across into the butter itself. The kernel figure is not the butter's figure.7

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Sunflower seed oil (Helianthus annuus, seed)
The bulk of the lipid phase. Conventional sunflower oil is linoleic-dominant; high-oleic cultivars invert that ratio, and the two are not interchangeable in the literature above. The fatty-acid profile of this material is not stated here, because it follows the cultivar and no analysis is held.
Jojoba wax esters (Simmondsia chinensis, seed)
Liquid wax ester, not a triglyceride. Simmondsia chinensis stands alone in its own family, Simmondsiaceae.
Caprylic/Capric Triglyceride
A medium-chain triglyceride carried in the lipid phase. No verified literature is held for this material; it therefore carries no property statement above.
Shea butter (Vitellaria paradoxa, kernel)
Declared under its INCI name, Butyrospermum Parkii Butter; Vitellaria paradoxa is the accepted botanical name and Butyrospermum parkii the synonym the ingredient register retains.
Polyglyceryl-3 Diisostearate
The emulsifier. It is what allows an anhydrous balm to take up water and rinse.
Tocopherol
Added to the lipid phase against oxidation.
Parfum
Fragrance. Limonene is declared separately, as EC 1223/2009 requires.

Full ingredients — INCI

Helianthus Annuus Seed Oil, Simmondsia Chinensis Seed Oil, Caprylic/Capric Triglyceride, Butyrospermum Parkii Butter, Polyglyceryl-3 Diisostearate, Tocopherol, Parfum, Limonene.

Declarable fragrance allergens

Limonene

Vessel
100 mL jar with a screw lid.
Recyclability
Scrape the jar out before disposal. Body and lid are separate parts and are best separated; check that your local stream accepts each.

Storage. Keep the lid closed, away from light and heat. The balm is anhydrous; keep water out of the jar and take it out with dry hands. Plant oils oxidise, and warmth hastens it.

Precautions. External use only. Avoid the eye area. Contains the declarable fragrance allergen limonene. Patch test before first use.
