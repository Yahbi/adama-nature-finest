How to use
Apply to clean, damp hair, from mid-length to ends. Leave five to ten minutes, then rinse thoroughly. Weekly.
  • Shikakai pod is recognised for its triterpenoid saponins — glycosides built on an acacic acid aglycone, carrying monoterpene and sugar units. Saponins are plant surfactants.1
  • Amla fruit is recognised for its hydrolysable tannins, phenolic acids and flavonoids, alongside ascorbic acid; more than 180 compounds have been isolated from the fruit.6
  • The roselle calyx is recognised for its anthocyanins, protocatechuic acid and organic acids — a different chemistry from the seed of the same plant.8
  • Shea butter is recognised for its stearic and oleic acids, carried principally as the triacylglycerol SOS, with a fraction of acetyl and cinnamyl triterpene esters reported between roughly 3.7 and 12.6 per cent of the fat.10

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Acacia concinna fruit extract — triterpenoid saponins
The dominant constituent class of the pod: acacic acid glycosides bearing monoterpene acyl moieties and five sugar units — glucose, xylose, rhamnose, quinovose, arabinose. Concinnosides and kinmoonosides have been isolated from this fraction. Named in the INCI register as Acacia Concinna Fruit Extract; the accepted botanical name is now Senegalia rugata.
Emblica officinalis fruit extract — hydrolysable tannins and polyphenols
The fruit carries tannins, phenolic acids, flavonoids, terpenoids and ascorbic acid. Emblica officinalis Gaertn. is a synonym; the accepted name is Phyllanthus emblica L., family Phyllanthaceae.
Hibiscus sabdariffa extract — anthocyanins and organic acids
Calyx constituents: anthocyanins, protocatechuic acid, phenolic acids, flavonoids, and organic acids including citric, malic and hibiscus acid. Reported composition varies by cultivar.
Butyrospermum parkii butter — stearic and oleic acids
Fatty acid balance varies by provenance: stearic-dominant in West African material, oleic-dominant in East African. Carries unsaponifiable matter, tocopherols, phytosterols, and polycyclic triterpene esters. Total polyphenols carried into the butter are reported at roughly 97 ppm — the higher kernel figures do not transfer to the butter. INCI retains Butyrospermum parkii; the accepted botanical name is Vitellaria paradoxa C.F.Gaertn.
Cetearyl alcohol, Behentrimonium methosulfate
The emulsion and conditioning base.
Benzyl alcohol, Dehydroacetic acid
The preservative system.

Vessel
200 mL jar
Recyclability
Empty and rinse the jar, and separate the lid, before presenting either for collection.

Storage. Keep the jar closed, away from heat and direct light. Take the product out with clean, dry hands; a jar is opened often, and water carried back into it shortens the life of any preservative system.

Precautions. External use. Rinse off. Avoid the eyes. Patch test on the inner forearm before first use. Two points on Acacia concinna are stated here plainly rather than left out. The Cosmetic Ingredient Review expert panel assessed Acacia Concinna Fruit Extract and concluded the available data were insufficient to support its safety in cosmetics, naming skin sensitisation and contact urticaria among the gaps (PMID 16422266). A case of occupational IgE-mediated respiratory allergy to shikakai powder is also on record, in a powder-handling context rather than a topical one (PMID 41386511). Neither finding closes the question in either direction. Anyone with a known sensitivity to the material should not use this mask.
