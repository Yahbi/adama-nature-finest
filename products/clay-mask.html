How to use
Apply an even layer to clean skin, leave 8–10 minutes, then rinse. One to two times weekly.
  • Illite is recognised for a particular architecture: a non-expanding 2:1 dioctahedral aluminosilicate, potassium held between the layers, iron carried as Fe(II) and Fe(III) within the octahedral sheet. It is that structure, and not a botanical chemistry, that the mineral is known by. The paper cited established the iron chemistry of these clays; it also reports non-expandable illite as the least antibacterial of the clays it tested, which is set down here rather than left out.1
  • An illite- and mica-dominant sediment characterised for cosmetic consideration was measured at a cation exchange capacity of 23.06 to 32.96 cmol per kilogram — a real, quantified capacity to hold and exchange ions — with its toxic-element levels below cosmetic-regulation limits. The authors state plainly that efficacy on skin remains to be evaluated. That sentence belongs on this page as much as the number does.2
  • Commercial clays whose principal minerals are kaolinite and illite — the two in this jar — have been characterised for oil-absorption capacity and for plasticity, the property by which a wet clay adheres to skin, with calcium as the main exchanged cation. The measurements are of the mineral, made in a veterinary preparation study, not of this formula.3
  • Kaolinite is recognised for being a hydrous aluminium phyllosilicate, Al2Si2O5(OH)4: one silica sheet bonded to one alumina sheet, stacked as pseudohexagonal platelets typically under two micrometres. Its faces carry opposite charges — the siloxane face negative, the aluminol face positive — which is why the platelet behaves as it does in water.4
  • The Cosmetic Ingredient Review expert panel concluded that kaolin is safe in cosmetics at present practices of use and concentration, and identified its function in a formula as absorbent and bulking agent. That is a function and a safety determination. The panel makes no efficacy claim, and neither do we.5
  • Aloe is recognised for a division within the leaf that matters here. The inner leaf gel — the material of Aloe Barbadensis Leaf Juice — carries the majority of the plant's reported bioactive compounds: polysaccharides, chiefly acetylated mannans, at some 3.82 to 6.55 g/kg, with phenolic acids and flavonoids alongside. The anthraquinones of the leaf latex are a separate fraction and are not what a decolorized inner-leaf juice is for. The review notes that well-designed controlled trials are still wanted before benefits are called substantiated.6
  • German chamomile is recognised for a well-characterised set of constituents: α-bisabolol and its oxides, the sesquiterpene lactone matricin, apigenin and its glucosides, and luteolin derivatives. Chamazulene — the blue of the distilled oil — is not present in the flower; it forms from matricin during steam distillation. This is an extract, not a distillate.7
  • Across the genus, composition is reported to vary with plant part, origin, genetics, harvest season and extraction method. That is the reason no percentage for α-bisabolol is printed on this page.8

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Illite
A non-expanding 2:1 dioctahedral potassium aluminosilicate, with structural iron in the octahedral sheet. Commonly co-occurs with chlorite, kaolinite and smectite. A mineral: no botanical name, no family.
Interlayer potassium and exchangeable cations
The exchange chemistry of the illite layer. Measured in illite-dominant sediment at 23.06–32.96 cmol/kg.
Kaolinite (INCI: Kaolin)
Hydrous aluminium silicate, Al2Si2O5(OH)4. A 1:1 layer phyllosilicate in pseudohexagonal platelets, typically under 2 µm, with a hydrophobic siloxane face and a hydrophilic aluminol face. Low cation exchange capacity. Named by the CIR panel as an absorbent and bulking agent.
Aloe inner leaf polysaccharides (acetylated mannans)
The characteristic fraction of the inner leaf gel, reported at some 3.82–6.55 g/kg. Distinct from the anthraquinones of the leaf latex, which belong to a different material.
α-Bisabolol and bisabolol oxides A and B
Terpene alcohols of Matricaria chamomilla.
Matricin
A sesquiterpene lactone of the flower. Chamazulene forms from it under steam distillation and is therefore absent from an extract.
Apigenin and its glucosides; luteolin derivatives
The flavonoids by which German chamomile is characterised.
Glycerin
Humectant.
Xanthan Gum
Polysaccharide thickener; holds the clay in suspension.
Sodium Benzoate and Potassium Sorbate
The preservative pair. A mask this wet requires one.

Full ingredients — INCI

Aqua, Illite (Green Clay), Kaolin, Glycerin, Aloe Barbadensis Leaf Juice, Chamomilla Recutita Extract, Xanthan Gum, Sodium Benzoate, Potassium Sorbate.

Vessel
100 mL jar
Recyclability
Empty and rinse the jar, and separate the lid, before presenting either for collection. Clay residue should go to waste rather than down the drain in quantity.

Storage. Keep the jar closed, away from heat and direct light. Take the product out with clean, dry hands. A jar is opened often, and water carried back into it shortens the life of any preservative system.

Precautions. External use. Rinse off. Avoid the eye area. Do not allow the mask to dry to cracking on the skin; rinse while it is still damp. Chamomile is an Asteraceae plant and a known sensitiser. Contact reactions to topical chamomile are reported, and anyone who reacts to mugwort, ragweed or daisy may cross-react (PMID 16628544). If that describes you, do not use this mask. Otherwise, patch test on the inner forearm before first use, and discontinue if irritation occurs.
