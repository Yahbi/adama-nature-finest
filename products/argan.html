In practice
One ingredient and no water, so nothing in the bottle needs preserving. It layers under a cream or over one, and it will sit beneath a hydrosol as easily as above it. On hair, it is a finishing oil on lengths and ends rather than a scalp treatment.
  • Argan kernel oil is recognised in the literature for a fatty-acid profile dominated by two unsaturated acids — oleic acid at roughly 45 to 48 per cent, and linoleic acid at roughly 31 per cent.2
  • The oil carries tocopherols, with gamma-tocopherol the predominant form — reported at some 687 mg/kg against about 51 mg/kg for alpha-tocopherol. Tocopherols are the vitamin E family.2
  • Two phytosterols, schottenol and spinasterol, are characteristic of argan and are reported alongside campesterol and polyphenols including ferulic acid. Few other plant oils carry this pair.2
  • In Morocco the oil has traditionally been used on skin and hair, a use documented across the ethnobotanical record from 1972 onward.1
  • Dermatological review of plant oils holds that the ratio of linoleic to oleic acid is a determinant of an oil's behaviour on the skin barrier, and that oils weighted toward oleic acid may not favour it. Argan is an oleic-weighted oil. We state the ratio rather than a conclusion.3

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Bibliography

  1. Mechqoq H, et al. Ethnobotany, phytochemistry and biological properties of Argan tree (Argania spinosa (L.) Skeels) (Sapotaceae) — A review. Journal of Ethnopharmacology, 2021. A narrative review of the literature from 1972 to 2021; establishes the family placement, the traditional Moroccan uses including skin and hair, and the oil's predominance of unsaturated fatty acids. Biological activities are collated from primary studies of varying quality, not demonstrated by the review itself. PubMed 34418509
  2. Rabbaa S, et al. Argan Oil: A Natural Bioactive Lipid Modulating Oxidative Stress and Inflammation. Antioxidants (Basel), 2025. A review characterising the oil's composition — oleic and linoleic acids, tocopherols, polyphenols, and the phytosterols schottenol and spinasterol. The antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects it describes are drawn from in-vitro and animal studies; the authors state that direct mechanistic evidence in humans remains limited. PubMed 40427397
  3. Vaughn AR, et al. Natural Oils for Skin-Barrier Repair: Ancient Compounds Now Backed by Modern Science. American Journal of Clinical Dermatology, 2018. A dermatology review of plant oils in topical use, argan among them; it holds the linoleic-to-oleic ratio to be a determinant of an oil's barrier behaviour. A review, not an original trial of argan oil. PubMed 28707186
  4. Barrientos N, et al. Allergic contact dermatitis caused by argan oil in an infant. Contact Dermatitis, 2014. A single case report, patch-test confirmed. It establishes that sensitisation to argan oil is possible and nothing about its frequency. It is listed here because a bibliography that carries only the favourable half of a literature is not a bibliography. PubMed 25345797

Principal constituents

Oleic acid
Roughly 45–48 per cent of the kernel oil's fatty acids.
Linoleic acid
Roughly 31 per cent of the kernel oil's fatty acids.
Gamma-tocopherol
The predominant tocopherol; about 687 mg/kg reported.
Alpha-tocopherol
A minor fraction; about 51 mg/kg reported.
Schottenol and spinasterol
Phytosterols close to distinctive of argan.
Campesterol
A further phytosterol of the oil.
Polyphenols
Including ferulic acid.
Triacylglycerols
The bulk lipid fraction.

Vessel
Dropper bottle, 50 mL
Material
Stoneware, with a dropper assembly
Recyclability
The stoneware is fired clay — kept and refilled rather than recycled. The dropper is a mixed-material assembly; separate it from the bottle before disposal.

Storage. Closed, upright, away from light and heat. An unsaturated oil oxidises; keep the cap on and use it within a reasonable time of opening.

Precautions. For external use. Argan oil is derived from a kernel; those who avoid tree nuts should approach it accordingly. A single published case report describes patch-test-confirmed allergic contact dermatitis to argan oil in an infant — one case says nothing of incidence, but sensitisation is possible, and a patch test on the inner forearm before first use is sensible. Avoid the eyes. (PMID 25345797)
