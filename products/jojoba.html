In practice
One material, undiluted, doing whatever you ask of it. Because it is a wax rather than a triglyceride, it belongs to a different chemical class from the seed oils it sits beside — which is the reason to keep it as a Simple and not blend it away.
  • Jojoba is not an oil in the ordinary sense. The seed stores its lipid reserve as liquid wax esters — long-chain fatty acids esterified to long-chain fatty alcohols — rather than as the triacylglycerols of every other seed oil in common use. The plant's genome and lipidome have been sequenced, and the pathways that direct wax ester accumulation identified.1
  • The species stands alone: Simmondsia chinensis is taxonomically isolated in its own monotypic family, Simmondsiaceae. There is no second plant like it in cosmetic use.1
  • The acid moieties of those esters are principally eicosenoic, erucic and oleic — reported at 55.5, 20.4 and 19.0 per cent respectively in one analysis of a single origin. A phenolic fraction accompanies them. The figures describe the sample analysed, not a species constant.2
  • Tocopherols and phytosterols are reported in the unsaponifiable fraction of the wax, alongside the fatty acid and fatty alcohol profile.3
  • Fatty acid and fatty alcohol composition has been compared across four jojoba varieties and found closely similar — the material is compositionally consistent between cultivars.4
  • In an ex vivo human skin organ culture — a laboratory model, not a trial in people — topical jojoba wax was reported to lower LPS-induced IL-6, IL-8 and TNF-α secretion and to raise collagen III and hyaluronic acid synthesis. The result belongs to the model. No clinical evidence is claimed for it, and none is held.3
  • The wax has a long record in dermatological and cosmetic formulation, on the strength of its wax-ester structure. The literature that record rests on is largely preclinical.5

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Liquid wax esters
The dominant fraction of the seed lipid, and the single most distinctive fact about this material. Long-chain fatty acids esterified to long-chain fatty alcohols — not a triglyceride oil.
Eicosenoic acid (C20:1)
Reported as the most abundant acid moiety of the esters.
Erucic acid (C22:1)
The second acid moiety by abundance in the analysis cited.
Oleic acid (C18:1)
The third acid moiety by abundance in the analysis cited.
Long-chain fatty alcohols
Eicosenol and docosenol — the alcohol half of the ester. Present because the material is a wax.
Tocopherols and phytosterols
Reported in the unsaponifiable fraction.
Phenolics
A minor fraction, reported in the seed oil.
Simmondsin
Named here to be excluded. The cyanoglucoside concentrates in the defatted seed cake, not in the cosmetic wax, and published work found the wax's activity independent of it. Nothing on this page is attributed to it.

Precautions. External use only. Avoid the eye area. Patch test before first use. The formula declares no fragrance and carries none of the 26 fragrance allergens declarable under EC 1223/2009. No antibacterial or purifying property is claimed: published testing found jojoba wax did not inhibit Staphylococcus aureus, Staphylococcus epidermidis or Malassezia at relevant concentrations.
