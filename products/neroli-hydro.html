Orange-Blossom Hydrosol

$24 200 mL

Neroli's gentler sister; the afternoon reset, unsweetened.

Bitter-orange blossoms distilled in season; the softest of the aromatic waters.

Mist over the face or through hair at any point in the day. Hold roughly 20 cm away. Avoid the eyes.
Dosage
Undiluted. Nothing to add.
In practice
A flower water is water first. It sits under an oil or a cream rather than over one, and it will not do the work of either. Used on hair, it is a light refresh between washes, not a conditioner. The bottle is water-based and therefore preserved: sodium benzoate and potassium sorbate need an acid base to function, so do not dilute or decant it.
  • By analogy with the essential oil drawn from the same blossom: gas chromatography of neroli oil resolves 33 compounds accounting for some 99 per cent of it, led by limonene at about 27.5 per cent, (E)-nerolidol at 17.5, alpha-terpineol at 14 and (E,E)-farnesol at 8. A flower water is the aqueous fraction of that distillation and not the oil; what it carries of these molecules has not been measured in the literature we hold.1
  • The blossom is recognised as the linalool-weighted part of this tree. Composition turns sharply on which part is distilled — the zest oil runs to 85 per cent limonene, the blossom does not. The flower and the peel are two materials from one plant.2
  • Citrus aurantium is among the more completely profiled species of the Rutaceae. Polarity-extended LC-MS metabolomics characterises 147 constituents across the plant — 92 of them reported for the species for the first time — and records that the profile shifts with where the tree grew.3

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Citrus Aurantium Amara Flower Water
The aqueous fraction of the blossom distillation. First on the list and almost all of it.
Limonene
The most abundant compound of the blossom oil, reported at about 27.5 per cent of it. Reported for the oil; not quantified in the water.
(E)-Nerolidol
A sesquiterpene alcohol; about 17.5 per cent of the blossom oil as reported.
Alpha-terpineol
A monoterpene alcohol; about 14 per cent of the blossom oil as reported.
(E,E)-Farnesol
About 8 per cent of the blossom oil as reported.
Linalool and linalyl acetate
Terpene alcohol and its ester, reported constituents of the blossom. We carry no figure for either that we can attribute, so we state them without one.
Sodium benzoate and potassium sorbate
The preservative pair. A water needs one; this is the plainest available and it is why the base is acidified.

Full ingredients — INCI

Citrus Aurantium Amara Flower Water, Sodium Benzoate, Potassium Sorbate.

Vessel
200 mL opaque stoneware cylinder with an atomiser.
Material
Stoneware body; black phenolic fine-mist atomiser with a dip tube, and an overcap.
Recyclability
The stoneware is fired clay — kept and refilled rather than recycled. The atomiser is a mixed assembly of plastic and a metal spring; separate it from the bottle before disposal.

Storage. Store closed, upright, away from direct light and heat. The stoneware is opaque, keeping light from the contents.

Precautions. For external use. Avoid the eye area. Discontinue if irritation occurs. Bitter orange is a Rutaceae and the literature carries allergy to citrus fruit in the ingestion setting; anyone with a known citrus sensitivity should patch test the inner forearm before first use. Cold-pressed citrus peel materials are commonly held to carry a phototoxicity question — this is a flower water and not a peel oil, and we hold no paper either way, so we state the distinction rather than a clearance.
