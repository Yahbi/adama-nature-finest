Aloe Vera Gel

$22 200 mL

Inner-leaf gel, cold-pressed; the first response to sun and razors.

Inner-leaf aloe, cold-pressed and stabilised, nothing to dilute it. After sun, after razors, after everything.

Key ingredients

How to use
Apply generously wherever skin is hot, tight or freshly shaved.
Botanical name
Aloe vera (L.) Burm.f.
Family
Asphodelaceae
Part used
Inner leaf
Function
Aqueous botanical juice; used undiluted or as the water phase of a formulation.
Presentation
200 mL pump
Also known as
Aloe barbadensis Miller, Barbados aloe, Curaçao aloe, True aloe, Aloès, Sábila
Suited to
All skin. Sun-exposed skin, shaved skin, skin that feels tight.
How to use
Apply generously wherever skin is hot, tight or freshly shaved. Leave to absorb; no rinse.
Dosage
Used undiluted, as supplied. As an ingredient in a formulation, aloe leaf juice is conventionally used as the aqueous phase, in whole or in part.
In practice
After sun. After razors. Under an oil, as the water a dry face is actually short of. The juice is the whole product — there is nothing here to dilute.
  • The inner leaf gel carries the majority of the plant's reported bioactive compounds. Its polysaccharide fraction — acetylated mannans, of which acemannan is the best described — has been quantified in the inner leaf gel at 3.82–6.55 g/kg, alongside phenolic acids at 274.5–307.5 mg/100 g and flavonoids at 3.63–4.70 g/kg.1
  • Polysaccharides and flavonoids are established as principal constituent classes of the plant, reported together with vitamins, minerals and amino acids.2
  • Aloe has a long record in cosmetology, documented in the dermatological literature. The same review states plainly that controlled trials are still needed to determine real efficacy; the tradition is old, the evidence is not settled.3

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Bibliography

  1. Kaur S, Bains K. Aloe Barbadensis Miller (Aloe Vera). International Journal for Vitamin and Nutrition Research, 2023. Narrative review reporting that the inner leaf gel carries the majority of the plant's bioactive compounds, with quantified ranges for polysaccharides, phenolic acids and flavonoids. The review states that further well-designed randomised controlled trials are needed to substantiate benefits. PubMed 37915246
  2. Abid A, Javed M, Zafar S, et al. The green healer: an updated review on the phytochemical profile and therapeutic potential of Aloe vera. Frontiers in Nutrition, 2025. Review of the phytochemical profile, establishing anthraquinones, flavonoids and polysaccharides as the principal reported constituent classes. Therapeutic sections are a synthesis of prior work, not primary evidence. PubMed 41098793
  3. Surjushe A, Vasani R, Saple DG. Aloe vera: a short review. Indian Journal of Dermatology, 2008. Dermatology review of the plant, its constituents and its uses in cosmetology. States explicitly that controlled trials are still needed to determine real efficacy. PubMed 19882025
  4. Burusapat C, Supawan M, Pruksapong C, et al. Topical Aloe Vera Gel for Accelerated Wound Healing of Split-Thickness Skin Graft Donor Sites: A Double-Blind, Randomized, Controlled Trial and Systematic Review. Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery, 2018. Small double-blind randomised trial (12 patients, 24 donor sites) in a surgical-wound setting; topical aloe vera gel shortened time to complete epithelialisation versus placebo. A graft donor site is not cosmetic skin and the result does not generalise to intact skin. PubMed 29649056
  5. Ufomadu P, Villar CF, Zhang SY, et al. A Comprehensive Systematic Review of Natural Biomedicines for Immune-Mediated and Inflammatory Dermatologic Diseases. Clinical Drug Investigation, 2025. PRISMA systematic review of 95 randomised controlled trials, in disease populations rather than cosmetic use. The authors caution that small sample sizes, design heterogeneity and inconsistent adverse-event reporting limit definitive conclusions. PubMed 40293693

Principal constituents

Polysaccharides (acetylated mannans)
The characteristic macromolecule of the inner leaf gel; reported at 3.82–6.55 g/kg.
Acemannan
An acetylated polymannose, the most studied member of the gel's polysaccharide fraction.
Phenolic acids
Reported in inner leaf gel at 274.5–307.5 mg/100 g.
Flavonoids
Reported at 3.63–4.70 g/kg.
Amino acids and minerals
Reported in nutritional composition analyses of the gel.

Full ingredients — INCI

Aloe Barbadensis Leaf Juice, Sodium Benzoate, Potassium Sorbate, Citric Acid.

Vessel
200 mL pump bottle
Material
The bottle is recyclable. The pump is a multi-material assembly — spring, dip tube, closure — and is not.
Recyclability
Separate the pump from the bottle before recycling; rinse the bottle. The pump goes to general waste, or keep it and refill.

Storage. Close after use. Keep away from direct light and heat. This is a water-based juice held by a preservative system of sodium benzoate and potassium sorbate at an acid pH; treat it as perishable and use it within a season of opening.

Precautions. External use only. Avoid the eyes. Discontinue if irritation appears. Cosmetic aloe is the decolorised inner-leaf gel: the leaf latex, which carries the anthraquinones aloin and aloe-emodin, is a separate material and is not what this is. The Cosmetic Ingredient Review record and the dermatological literature both note that the efficacy evidence for aloe on intact skin is thinner than its reputation; the strongest controlled trial in this bibliography was conducted on surgical graft donor sites, not on cosmetic skin.
