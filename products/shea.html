Raw Shea Butter

$18 200 g

Unrefined, nutty, faintly smoked; the whole-food of the body ritual.

Village-milled in Ghana and never refined, so everything stays: the vitamins, the faint smoke, the character. One-ingredient body care.

In practice
One ingredient and no water, so nothing in the jar needs preserving. It is a solid at room temperature and softens against the skin rather than in the air; the palms do the work. It takes longer to settle than an oil does — allow it a minute before dressing. On hair it belongs to the lengths and ends, not the scalp.
  • Shea butter is recognised in the literature for a lipid fraction built on two fatty acids — stearic and oleic — and for a principal triacylglycerol, SOS, in which one oleic acid sits between two stearic. Which of the two fatty acids leads is not fixed: across 150 samples from four African countries, stearic acid predominated in West African material and oleic acid in Ugandan.2
  • The butter carries acetyl and cinnamyl polycyclic triterpene esters, reported at country means of roughly 3.7 to 12.6 per cent of the fat. This triterpene ester fraction is a large one by the standards of plant butters, and it is the constituent class most particular to shea.2
  • Alongside the fat sit unsaponifiable matter, tocopherols — the vitamin E family — and phytosterols. Refining is reported to lower each of them; the crude butter carries more.3
  • The kernel is rich in catechins — eight of them identified by LC-MS, at a mean of some 4,000 ppm of kernel dry weight, together with gallic acid, quercetin and trans-cinnamic acid. Little of that carries through: total polyphenols extracted into the butter itself are reported at a mean of about 97 ppm, in a range of 62 to 135. We state the butter's figure, not the kernel's.1
  • Shea butter is among the vegetable butters that have been examined clinically for dermal use — on unaffected skin, on xerosis, and on atopic dermatitis. A review of twenty-seven such studies across seventeen oils draws its conclusions at the level of fatty-acid class, oleic against linoleic, rather than for shea in particular. That is the honest limit of what has been shown.4

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Bibliography

  1. Maranz S, Wiesman Z, Garti N. Phenolic constituents of shea (Vitellaria paradoxa) kernels. Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry, 2003. An LC-MS characterisation identifying eight catechins together with gallic acid, quercetin and trans-cinnamic acid in the kernels; it quantifies mean kernel catechin content at some 4,000 ppm and the total polyphenols extracted into the butter at about 97 ppm. Composition only. No biological or skin activity was tested. PubMed 14518954
  2. Di Vincenzo D, Maranz S, Serraiocco A, et al. Regional variation in shea butter lipid and triterpene composition in four African countries. Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry, 2005. A compositional survey of 150 samples from Mali, Burkina Faso, Nigeria and Uganda, establishing the triacylglycerol, fatty-acid and polycyclic triterpene profile, and that stearic acid predominates in West Africa where oleic predominates in Uganda. Analytical chemistry; it makes no functional claim. PubMed 16159175
  3. Abdel-Razek AG, Abo-Elwafa GA, Al-Amrousi EF, et al. Effect of Refining and Fractionation Processes on Minor Components, Fatty Acids, Antioxidant and Antimicrobial Activities of Shea Butter. Foods, 2023. Confirms stearic and oleic as the two principal fatty acids and quantifies the unsaponifiable, tocopherol, phytosterol, phenolic and flavonoid content of crude butter, reporting that refining lowers each. The antioxidant and antimicrobial activity it measures is in vitro — not in skin, and not in people. PubMed 37107421
  4. Poljšak N, Kočevar Glavač N. Vegetable Butters and Oils as Therapeutically and Cosmetically Active Ingredients for Dermal Use: A Review of Clinical Studies. Frontiers in Pharmacology, 2022. A narrative review of twenty-seven clinical studies across seventeen vegetable oils, shea among them, covering unaffected skin, xerosis and atopic dermatitis. Its conclusions are general to fatty-acid class rather than specific to shea, and it is a review, not primary evidence for any claim made of this butter. PubMed 35548366

Principal constituents

Stearic acid
One of the two principal fatty acids; predominant in West African material.
Oleic acid
The other; predominant in East African material.
Triacylglycerols
The bulk lipid fraction, principally SOS — 1,3-distearoyl-2-oleoyl-glycerol.
Acetyl and cinnamyl polycyclic triterpene esters
Country means of roughly 3.7–12.6 per cent of the fat.
Unsaponifiable matter
Quantified in crude butter; reduced by refining.
Tocopherols
The vitamin E family. Reduced by refining.
Phytosterols
Reported in the crude butter; reduced by refining.
Polyphenols
About 97 ppm in the butter, in a range of 62–135. Far higher in the kernel than in the fat pressed from it.

Full ingredients — INCI

Butyrospermum Parkii Butter.

Vessel
200 g jar with a screw lid.
Recyclability
Scrape the jar out before disposal. Body and lid are separate parts and are best separated; check that your local stream accepts each.

Storage. Closed, upright, away from light and heat. It will soften and re-set with the room; that is the material, not a fault. Keep the lid on and take it out with clean, dry hands.

Precautions. For external use. Shea butter is derived from a nut; those who avoid tree nuts should approach it accordingly. A patch test on the inner forearm before first use is sensible. Avoid the eyes.
