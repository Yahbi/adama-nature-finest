Solid, sulphate-free hair cleanser.
Also known as
Shea, Shea butter tree, Karité, Butyrospermum parkii (synonym; still the INCI-recognised name), Butyrospermum paradoxum, Beurre de karité, Nku (Bambara), Ori (Yoruba)
Suited to
Normal to dry hair and scalp.
How to use
Rub the bar between wet hands or directly onto wet hair, lather, then rinse. Stand the bar somewhere it can drain between uses.
  • Shea butter is recognised for a fat dominated by stearic and oleic acids, principally as the triacylglycerol SOS, with acetyl and cinnamyl polycyclic triterpene esters reported at country means of 3.7–12.6 per cent of the fat. The balance shifts with provenance: West African shea runs stearic-dominant and firm, East African shea oleic-dominant and soft.1
  • Crude shea butter is recognised for a minor fraction — unsaponifiable matter, tocopherols, phytosterols and phenolics — that refining measurably lowers.3
  • The shea kernel is recognised for its catechins, reported at a mean near 4000 ppm of kernel dry weight, alongside gallic acid, quercetin and trans-cinnamic acid. Little of this crosses into the butter: total polyphenols carried through are reported at a mean of 97 ppm.2
  • Argan kernel oil is recognised for oleic acid at roughly 45–48 per cent and linoleic acid at roughly 31 per cent of its fatty acids, a gamma-dominant tocopherol load, and two phytosterols — schottenol and spinasterol — that are close to particular to this tree.4
  • Rosemary is recognised for an essential oil built chiefly on 1,8-cineole, α-pinene and camphor; some 150 compounds have been reported across samples of it. One analysis of a Moroccan oil placed 1,8-cineole at 28 per cent and α-pinene at 27 per cent, but rosemary runs in chemotypes and no single figure holds across origins.6

The properties above are drawn from the published literature cited below. They describe the constituents of the ingredients, not a tested effect of this product.

Principal constituents

Sodium Cocoyl Isethionate and Coco-Glucoside
The two surfactants. They carry the cleansing and the lather; neither is a sulphate.
Stearic and oleic acids
The two principal fatty acids of shea butter, largely as the triacylglycerol SOS. Which of the two leads depends on where the butter comes from.
Acetyl and cinnamyl triterpene esters
Shea's distinctive unsaponifiable fraction, reported at country means of 3.7–12.6 per cent of the fat.
Oleic and linoleic acids
The fatty acids of argan kernel oil, reported at roughly 45–48 and 31 per cent respectively.
Schottenol and spinasterol
The two phytosterols of argan oil. They are the most particular thing in it.
1,8-Cineole and α-pinene
The constituents most frequently reported as dominant in rosemary leaf oil, with camphor. Proportions vary by chemotype.
Tocopherol
Vitamin E. Present in the shea and argan fractions, and added.
Citric Acid
pH adjuster.

Full ingredients — INCI

Sodium Cocoyl Isethionate, Coco-Glucoside, Butyrospermum Parkii Butter, Argania Spinosa Kernel Oil, Rosmarinus Officinalis Leaf Oil, Citric Acid, Tocopherol.

Vessel
100 g bar
Material
None. The product is the bar; it needs no container to hold its shape.
Recyclability
Nothing to recycle and nothing to separate — the reason the format exists.

Storage. Keep the bar dry between uses; standing water will soften it. Store away from heat and direct light.

Precautions. For external use. Avoid the eyes; rinse if the lather reaches them. The bar contains rosemary leaf oil — an essential oil whose principal constituents are 1,8-cineole and camphor — and the literature carries a patch-test-confirmed case report of allergic contact dermatitis to argan oil, so sensitisation to either is possible. A case report says nothing about how often it happens. Discontinue if irritation occurs.
